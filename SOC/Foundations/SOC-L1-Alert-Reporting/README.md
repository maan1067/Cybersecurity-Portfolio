## Alert Funnel

Security alerts follow a structured workflow before becoming confirmed security incidents. Most alerts generated by security monitoring systems are investigated and closed by SOC Level 1 analysts, while only a small percentage require escalation to higher-level analysts.

This process is commonly referred to as the **Alert Funnel**, where a large volume of alerts is gradually reduced to a small number of confirmed incidents.

### Alert Funnel Workflow

```text
Security Events
        │
        ▼
Alert Generation
        │
        ▼
SOC Level 1 Alert Triage
        │
 ┌──────┴────────┐
 │               │
 ▼               ▼
False Positive   True Positive
(Closed)             │
                     ▼
             Alert Reporting
                     │
                     ▼
             Alert Escalation
                     │
                     ▼
              SOC Level 2
                     │
                     ▼
        Incident Response / DFIR
```

## Alert Reporting

Alert reporting is the process of documenting the investigation performed by the SOC analyst.

A report should clearly explain:

- The alert summary.
- Investigation steps performed.
- Evidence collected.
- Analysis results.
- Final verdict.
- Recommendations or next actions.

Detailed reporting is especially important for True Positive alerts because it provides valuable context for the next analyst handling the case.

---

## Alert Escalation

Alert escalation is the process of transferring a confirmed security alert to a higher-level analyst when additional investigation or remediation is required.

Escalation is typically performed when:

- The alert represents a confirmed security threat.
- Additional technical expertise is required.
- Containment or remediation actions are necessary.
- The investigation exceeds the responsibilities of a SOC Level 1 analyst.

A complete investigation report should always accompany the escalation to reduce investigation time for the receiving analyst.

---

## Communication

SOC analysts frequently communicate with other internal teams during investigations.

Examples include:

- Contacting the IT department to verify administrative activities.
- Communicating with HR to validate user information.
- Coordinating with system administrators regarding affected servers.
- Requesting additional information from asset owners.

Effective communication improves investigation accuracy and accelerates incident response.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Most alerts are resolved by SOC Level 1 analysts.
- Only confirmed or complex threats should be escalated.
- High-quality reporting improves investigation continuity.
- Effective communication is essential for successful incident response.

## Reporting Guide

Writing alert reports is an important responsibility for SOC Level 1 analysts. A well-documented report provides valuable context for future investigations, improves communication between security teams, and helps analysts develop stronger analytical skills.

### Why Alert Reporting Matters

| Purpose | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| Provide Context for Escalation | A detailed report allows SOC Level 2 analysts to quickly understand the investigation without starting from scratch. |
| Preserve Investigation Findings | SIEM logs may be retained for a limited period, while alert records are often stored for much longer. Documenting findings inside the alert preserves important context. |
| Improve Analyst Skills | Writing reports forces analysts to summarize evidence, explain reasoning, and validate their conclusions. |

---

## Recommended Report Format — The Five Ws

A practical reporting approach is to answer the Five Ws:

### Who
Identify the user, account, process owner, or entity involved in the alert.

### What
Describe the suspicious activity or sequence of events that occurred.

### When
Specify the start and end time of the activity, including relevant timestamps.

### Where
Identify the affected host, IP address, domain, cloud resource, or website associated with the alert.

### Why
Explain the reasoning behind the final verdict and why the activity was classified as a True Positive or False Positive.

---

## Example Report Structure

```text
Who: User ahmed on workstation FIN-WS-12
What: Executed an encoded PowerShell command that downloaded a file from an external domain
When: 2026-07-16 10:32 UTC
Where: Host FIN-WS-12, IP 10.10.5.24
Why: The command contacted a known malicious domain and matched the organization's PowerShell abuse detection rule. Classified as True Positive.
```

---

## Key Takeaways

- Reports should provide enough context for another analyst to continue the investigation.
- The Five Ws help ensure that critical information is not missed.
- Clear reporting improves escalation efficiency and investigation quality.
- Good report writing is a core SOC Level 1 skill.

## SOC Communication

Effective communication is a critical component of Security Operations Center (SOC) workflows. In addition to investigating alerts and performing escalations, analysts must communicate efficiently with teammates and other departments to ensure incidents are handled without unnecessary delays.

Most organizations define **Crisis Communication Procedures**, which specify how analysts should respond during critical situations, system failures, or high-priority security incidents.

---

## Common Communication Scenarios

### Scenario 1 — L2 Analyst Is Unavailable

If a critical alert requires immediate escalation but the assigned SOC Level 2 analyst is unavailable:

- Attempt to contact the on-duty L2 analyst.
- Escalate to the SOC Level 3 analyst if necessary.
- Notify the SOC Manager if no senior analyst is available.

Critical incidents should never remain unattended.

---

### Scenario 2 — Compromised Communication Account

If an alert indicates that a user's communication platform (such as Microsoft Teams or Slack) has been compromised:

- Do not communicate with the user through the compromised platform.
- Use an alternative communication channel such as:
  - Phone call
  - Corporate email (if trusted)
  - Face-to-face communication

Using a compromised account could expose sensitive information to the attacker.

---

### Scenario 3 — High Alert Volume

During periods of unusually high alert volume:

- Continue prioritizing alerts based on severity.
- Focus first on Critical and High alerts.
- Inform the on-duty SOC Level 2 analyst about the increased workload.
- Request additional support if necessary.

Maintaining communication prevents important alerts from being overlooked.

---

### Scenario 4 — Incorrect Alert Classification

If an analyst later discovers that an alert was incorrectly classified:

- Immediately notify the SOC Level 2 analyst.
- Explain the original investigation.
- Share the newly discovered evidence.
- Continue supporting the investigation if requested.

Prompt reporting helps reduce the impact of missed threats.

---

### Scenario 5 — SIEM Issues

If SIEM logs are incomplete, unavailable, or improperly parsed:

- Investigate using any available information.
- Do not ignore the alert.
- Report the issue to the SOC Engineer or SOC Level 2 analyst.
- Document the limitation within the investigation report.

Monitoring infrastructure issues should always be reported.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Effective communication is essential for successful incident response.
- Critical incidents must always be escalated without delay.
- Alternative communication channels should be used when user accounts are compromised.
- Analysts should immediately report investigation mistakes.
- SIEM or logging issues should never result in skipped investigations.

---

## Conclusion

Alert reporting, escalation, and communication are fundamental responsibilities of a SOC Level 1 analyst. These activities ensure that security investigations are properly documented, critical incidents receive timely attention, and collaboration between security teams remains effective.

High-quality reporting preserves investigation context, proper escalation enables advanced incident response, and clear communication ensures efficient coordination across technical and business teams.
